Coil Cable vs Coiled Cord: A Terminology Guide for Buyers

Why the same product goes by different names, what the engineering distinctions actually mean, and how to specify coil cables correctly when sourcing.

Published November 15, 2024 10 min read

What Is a Coil Cable?

A coil cable is an electrical conductor assembly wound into a helical (spiral) shape that stretches under tension and returns to its compact resting form when released. The helical geometry is permanently set into the cable's jacket material through a heat-curing process, giving it elastic spring memory without any mechanical parts. Pull a coil cable to the length you need and it stays extended under gentle tension. Release it and it retracts on its own.

If you have ever used a telephone handset cord, plugged in a power tool on a workshop bench, or connected a microphone to a dispatch radio, you have used a coil cable. The product category spans everything from 26 AWG signal lines carrying milliamps to 10 AWG power cables rated at 600 volts and 30 amps. What unifies them is the coiled construction and the self-retracting behavior it produces.

Buyers searching for coil cables will encounter a wide range of names for what is fundamentally the same product family. Coiled cable, coiled cord, retractile cord, curly cord, spiral cable, and coiling cable all appear in supplier catalogs, specification sheets, and purchasing databases. Understanding the terminology is the first step toward sourcing the right product, and that is what this guide covers.

Cable vs. Cord vs. Wire: What the Terms Actually Mean

Before addressing coil-specific terminology, it helps to understand how the broader electrical industry defines its basic terms. These definitions come from the National Electrical Code (NEC) and UL standards, and they carry real engineering and compliance implications.

Wire

A wire is a single metallic conductor, either solid or stranded. A bare copper wire is just the metal itself. An insulated wire adds a layer of thermoplastic or thermoset insulation around the conductor. Wire is the fundamental building block. You do not buy a "coil wire" as a finished product — wire is what goes inside a cable or cord assembly.

Cable

A cable is two or more insulated conductors grouped together inside a common outer jacket or sheath. The term implies a more permanent installation context. Cables are often run through conduit, cable trays, or raceways. In the coil cable context, the word "cable" tends to appear when the product carries data, signal, or control circuits — or when the buyer comes from an engineering or telecommunications background where "cable" is the default vocabulary.

Cord

A cord is a flexible cable assembly designed for temporary or portable connections. Cords use finely stranded conductors for flexibility, and their jackets are engineered to withstand repeated bending, coiling, and movement. Under NEC and UL classifications, portable power products are formally designated as "cords" — hence cord type designations like SJT, SO, SOW, and SJTO. A retractile cord is the formal UL product category for what most people call a coil cable.

The practical takeaway: when someone searches for a "coil cable," they are almost always looking for what the industry formally classifies as a retractile cord. The product is the same. The terminology difference reflects whether the buyer thinks in cable engineering terms or cord product terms.

Why Different Industries Use Different Terms

The terminology fragmentation is not random. It follows industry lines, and understanding why helps buyers communicate more effectively with suppliers.

Telecommunications established "curly cord" and "coiled cord" as everyday vocabulary because the telephone handset cord was the most visible coil cable product for decades. Anyone who grew up with a landline phone knows the term intuitively.

Electrical engineering and power distribution professionals tend to use "retractile cord" because that is the term in UL standards, the NEC, and formal electrical specifications. When an engineer writes a spec for a hospital or manufacturing facility, "retractile cord" is what appears on the drawing.

OEM product designers and electronics engineers often search for "coil cable" or "coiled cable" because "cable" is the default term in their vocabulary. A product designer specifying a coiled USB cable, a shielded signal cable for a medical device, or a multi-conductor control cable for a robotic arm thinks in cable terms, not cord terms.

European and international buyers frequently use "spiral cable" (Spiralkabel in German, câble spirale in French) because IEC standards and European catalogs favor that terminology.

Purchasing and procurement teams use whatever term their internal system has standardized on, which could be any of the above. This is why manufacturers like Autac cross-reference all common terms in our catalog and product database — because a buyer searching for "coil cables" and a buyer searching for "retractile cords" need to find the same products.

Types of Coil Cables

The coil cable family divides into distinct categories based on what the cable carries and how it is constructed. Understanding these categories matters more than terminology when it comes to sourcing the right product.

Power Coil Cables

These carry AC or DC power to equipment and tools. They use larger conductors (10–16 AWG), heavy-duty jacket materials, and are UL-listed under cord type designations like SJT (300V, indoor), SJTO (300V, oil-resistant), SO (600V, industrial), and SOW (600V, water and oil-resistant). Power coil cables are the workhorses of the product family — used on everything from workshop power tools to hospital bed controls to commercial kitchen equipment.

Signal and Data Coil Cables

These carry low-voltage signals for communication, control, and data transfer. Conductor gauges range from 18 AWG to 26 AWG, with conductor counts from 2 to 25 or more. Applications include PLC connections, sensor leads, serial data links, USB connections, barcode scanner tethers, and intercom wiring. Signal coil cables prioritize conductor count and signal integrity over current-carrying capacity.

Shielded Coil Cables

Any coil cable — power or signal — can be manufactured with electromagnetic shielding. Shield types include braided copper, spiral-wound copper, and foil wraps. Shielded coil cables are essential in medical environments (where EMI can affect patient monitoring equipment), audio/video production (where interference creates noise), and industrial automation (where variable frequency drives and motors generate electromagnetic interference). Shielding adds diameter and stiffness, which slightly reduces the extension ratio.

Multi-Conductor Coil Cables

Coil cables with four or more conductors serve applications that need multiple independent circuits in a single retractile assembly. Examples include pendant controllers for overhead cranes (which need circuits for up, down, left, right, and emergency stop), dispatch consoles (voice, push-to-talk, and signaling on separate conductors), and robotic tool changers. Autac manufactures multi-conductor coil cables with up to 25 conductors in a single assembly.

Miniature Coil Cables

Compact coil cables with small outer diameters, typically using 22–26 AWG conductors in lightweight jacket materials. These are designed for handheld and portable devices where weight, bulk, and flexibility are primary concerns: barcode scanners, test probes, wearable medical devices, and portable instrumentation. Miniature coil cables often use polypropylene or thin-wall PVC jackets to minimize coil diameter.

How Coil Cables Are Manufactured

The manufacturing process is what separates a coil cable from a straight cable. Understanding the process helps buyers appreciate why coil cables are engineered products, not simply straight cables that have been wound up.

Step 1: Conductor and Cable Production

The process begins with standard cable manufacturing. Copper conductors are drawn to the specified AWG gauge and stranded for flexibility. Individual conductors are insulated, then cabled (twisted together in a specific lay pattern). If shielding is required, it is applied over the cabled conductors. Finally, the outer jacket is extruded over the assembly. At this stage, the cable is straight and looks like any other flexible cable.

Step 2: Mandrel Winding

The straight cable is wound tightly around a steel mandrel (a cylindrical form) at a precise pitch and tension. The mandrel diameter determines the coil's inner diameter, and the winding pitch determines how tightly the coils sit against each other in the retracted state. This step requires careful control — inconsistent winding produces coil cables that do not retract evenly or that develop uneven tension spots.

Step 3: Heat Setting (Curing)

The wound mandrel assembly goes into an oven where it is heat-cured at a specific temperature and duration calibrated to the jacket material. This is the critical step. The heat permanently realigns the polymer chains in the jacket material, setting the helical shape at the molecular level. PVC, TPE, polyurethane, and neoprene each require different curing profiles. Autac's proprietary Auta-Prene compound is specifically formulated for superior heat-set memory and long-term retractile performance.

Step 4: Cooling and Removal

After curing, the mandrel assembly is cooled in a controlled manner. Rapid cooling can stress the jacket material and degrade retractile performance. Once cooled, the coil cable is removed from the mandrel. It now holds its helical shape permanently and will return to it after being stretched, thousands of times over its service life.

Step 5: Termination and Testing

Tangent leads (the straight sections at each end of the coil) are left or added to provide strain relief and connection points. Plugs, connectors, or bare leads are attached depending on the application. Every coil cable is tested for electrical continuity, insulation resistance, and retractile performance before shipping.

Key Specifications When Sourcing Coil Cables

Whether you call it a coil cable, coiled cable, or retractile cord, the specifications that matter are the same. Here are the parameters to define when requesting a quote or searching a catalog.

  • Wire gauge (AWG) — Determines current-carrying capacity. Ranges from 10 AWG (30 amps, heavy industrial) to 26 AWG (1 amp, miniature signal). Heavier gauge means stiffer coil and larger diameter.
  • Number of conductors — 2-conductor for simple circuits, 3-conductor for grounded power, 4+ conductors for control, data, and multi-circuit applications. Available up to 25 conductors.
  • Voltage rating — 300V for SJT-type light-duty power cables. 600V for SO/SOW heavy-duty industrial cables. Signal cables may be rated at 30V, 150V, or 300V depending on the application standard.
  • Jacket material — PVC for indoor and light-duty use. TPE for moderate chemical and temperature exposure. Polyurethane for abrasion resistance. Neoprene or Auta-Prene for oil, chemical, UV, and extreme temperature environments.
  • Retracted length — The compact resting length of the coiled section, typically 1 to 10 feet. This determines your storage footprint.
  • Extended length — The maximum working reach, approximately 5x the retracted length. A 2-foot retracted coil cable extends to roughly 10 feet. A 6-foot retracted cable reaches about 30 feet.
  • Tangent lead length — The straight (non-coiled) sections at each end, typically 6 to 12 inches. These provide strain relief and mounting flexibility at connection points.
  • Shielding — None, braided copper, spiral copper, or foil. Required for EMI-sensitive environments. Specify the shielding coverage percentage if signal integrity is critical.
  • UL listing — UL and cUL listing certifies compliance with safety standards. The UL category for retractile cords is ZJCZ. Power coil cables for commercial use should always be UL-listed.
  • Termination — Molded plugs (NEMA 5-15P, 6-15P, etc.), connectors, bare stripped leads, or custom terminations. Specify both ends.

Coil Cable Applications by Industry

The versatility of coil cables is reflected in the range of industries that depend on them. Here is where different types of coil cables see the heaviest use.

Manufacturing and Industrial Automation

Assembly lines use coil cables to connect handheld tools, pendant controllers, barcode scanners, and sensor arrays to fixed stations. The retractile design keeps cables out of moving machinery, off conveyor paths, and away from robotic work cells. Heavy-duty SO and SOW coil cables in 10–14 AWG handle motor-driven tools. Multi-conductor coil cables connect crane pendants and control panels.

Healthcare and Medical Devices

Patient monitors, infusion pumps, diagnostic carts, and examination lights use coil cables to maintain clean, trip-free patient care environments. Shielded coil cables protect signal integrity for sensitive monitoring equipment. Hospital procurement teams typically specify UL-listed, shielded coil cables with medical-grade jacket materials.

Telecommunications and Networking

The original coil cable application. Beyond telephone handset cords, modern telecom uses coil cables for dispatch console microphones, headset connections, intercom systems, and rack-to-equipment patch connections where cable management in tight spaces matters.

Automotive and Transportation

Trailer-to-tractor electrical connections, EV charging station tethers, and in-vehicle equipment connections all use coil cables. The retractile design accommodates the relative movement between a cab and trailer during turns, and the self-retracting behavior prevents cable drag on road surfaces. Automotive coil cables require jackets rated for UV exposure, wide temperature ranges, and oil resistance.

Point-of-Sale and Retail

POS terminals, payment devices, customer-facing displays, and barcode scanners connected by coil cables stay organized on checkout counters. Miniature coil cables with 22–26 AWG conductors and USB or proprietary connectors keep counter surfaces clear without restricting cashier reach.

Audio, Video, and Broadcast

Camera tethers, microphone cables, headphone extensions, and intercom connections in broadcast studios and live production environments use shielded coil cables to prevent electromagnetic interference. The retractile behavior lets camera operators and sound engineers move freely within a controlled radius without cable tangles on studio floors.

Test and Measurement

Bench test equipment, handheld multimeters, oscilloscope probes, and portable analyzers use miniature coil cables to connect probes and accessories. The retractile design prevents probe cables from draping across live circuits on the bench, reducing the risk of accidental shorts and measurement errors.

How to Source Coil Cables

Sourcing coil cables effectively requires knowing what to specify, where to look, and what to ask potential suppliers.

Start with Specifications, Not Terminology

Define your electrical requirements (AWG, conductors, voltage, shielding) and mechanical requirements (retracted length, extended length, jacket material, terminations) before contacting suppliers. A clear specification eliminates terminology confusion. Whether you call it a coil cable or a retractile cord, the spec sheet speaks the same language.

Catalog vs. Custom

Most coil cable manufacturers maintain a catalog of standard configurations — common combinations of gauge, conductor count, length, and jacket material. Autac's catalog includes over 400 standard part numbers. If your application matches a standard configuration, you benefit from shorter lead times and proven designs. If it does not, custom coil cables can be manufactured to exact specifications. Custom is common in this market; it is not a special request.

Domestic vs. Offshore Manufacturing

Coil cables from domestic manufacturers like Autac offer advantages in quality control, lead time predictability, communication speed, and compliance traceability. UL listing, material certifications, and lot traceability are straightforward to verify with a domestic manufacturer. For applications where safety certification and supply chain reliability matter — healthcare, industrial automation, government contracts — domestic sourcing reduces risk.

What to Ask Your Supplier

  • Is the product UL-listed? Under which category (ZJCZ for retractile cords)?
  • What jacket material is used, and what is its temperature range?
  • What is the actual extended-to-retracted ratio for this specific configuration?
  • What is the cycle life expectancy (how many extend/retract cycles before performance degrades)?
  • Can you provide lot traceability and material certifications?
  • What are lead times for standard catalog items vs. custom configurations?
  • Do you offer sample quantities for testing before committing to production volumes?
