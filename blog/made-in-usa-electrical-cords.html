Made in USA Electrical Cords: Why Domestic Manufacturing Matters

The practical procurement advantages of American-made power cords and retractile cords — from compliance and quality control to lead times and supply chain resilience.

Published October 15, 2025 10 min read

The Case for Domestic Sourcing

When procurement teams evaluate electrical cord suppliers, price per unit tends to dominate the conversation. That makes sense on a spreadsheet. But anyone who has managed a production line, maintained a fleet of medical devices, or outfitted a government facility knows that the total cost of an electrical cord extends well beyond the line-item price. Lead time uncertainty, compliance documentation, quality inconsistency, and communication friction all carry real costs — costs that rarely appear on the purchase order but show up in delayed projects, rejected shipments, and rework hours.

This is not an argument against global trade. Overseas manufacturing serves an important role in many product categories. But electrical cords occupy a specific space where domestic manufacturing offers measurable, practical advantages that buyers should weigh against unit cost. The reasons are rooted in regulatory complexity, supply chain mechanics, and the nature of custom electrical products.

At Autac, we have manufactured retractile cords and coiled power cords in North Branford, Connecticut since 1947. Two generations of the Burkle family have run this operation. We have watched the cord industry shift overseas, and we have watched portions of it come back. The patterns are consistent, and they come down to a handful of factors that matter more than most buyers initially expect.

Quality Control You Can Verify

Electrical cords are safety-critical components. A power cord that fails under load, overheats due to substandard conductor gauge, or uses a jacket material that does not meet its rated flame resistance creates real hazard — not just liability exposure, but physical danger to the people using the equipment it powers.

When you source made in USA electrical cords, the entire manufacturing process operates under U.S. regulatory oversight. UL (Underwriters Laboratories) conducts follow-up inspections at domestic manufacturing facilities as part of its listing program. OSHA standards govern the workplace. The raw materials — copper conductors, jacket compounds, molded plugs — come from suppliers who operate under the same regulatory framework and can be audited directly.

This does not mean that every imported cord is substandard. Many overseas factories produce excellent products. But the verification chain is longer and more difficult to inspect. When a quality issue surfaces with a domestic supplier, you are dealing with a company in the same time zone, under the same legal system, with a reputation in its local industry. The feedback loop between identifying a problem and resolving it is measured in days, not weeks or months.

At Autac, every cord we manufacture is tested before it ships. Our quality control process is not a certificate posted on a website — it is people on a production floor in Connecticut inspecting product that carries our name. When we say a cord is UL-listed and rated for a specific voltage, amperage, and temperature range, that claim is backed by testing that happens in the same building where the cord was made.

Compliance: Buy American Act, Berry Amendment, and TAA

For buyers in government, defense, and federally funded sectors, domestic sourcing is not just a preference — it is a legal requirement. Three federal mandates directly affect electrical cord procurement:

The Buy American Act (BAA)

The Buy American Act requires federal agencies to give preference to domestically manufactured products when spending federal funds. For manufactured goods like electrical cords, the product must be manufactured in the United States and the cost of domestic components must exceed a specified threshold of the total cost. The thresholds have been progressively tightened through executive orders in recent years, making BAA compliance increasingly difficult for products assembled from predominantly imported components.

For procurement officers at federal agencies, military installations, VA hospitals, and federally funded research facilities, sourcing American made power cords from a domestic manufacturer simplifies compliance documentation significantly. The manufacturer can provide a straightforward BAA compliance letter because the manufacturing, materials, and labor are genuinely domestic.

The Berry Amendment

The Berry Amendment goes further than the BAA. It requires the Department of Defense to give preference to domestically grown, reprocessed, reused, or produced items — including textiles, clothing, and certain manufactured goods. While not all electrical cords fall under Berry Amendment requirements, those used in military applications, defense facilities, and DOD-funded projects may be subject to its provisions. Sourcing from a U.S. manufacturer with domestic raw materials provides the clearest path to Berry Amendment compliance.

The Trade Agreements Act (TAA)

For purchases made through the GSA Schedule and other federal contract vehicles, the Trade Agreements Act requires that products be manufactured or substantially transformed in the United States or in a designated TAA-compliant country. Products manufactured in non-designated countries — which includes several major cord-producing nations — are not eligible for GSA Schedule purchases. A domestic manufacturer eliminates any ambiguity about TAA compliance.

The compliance burden is not trivial. Procurement officers who source electrical cords internationally must verify country of origin, calculate domestic content percentages, and maintain documentation for audit purposes. A single non-compliant purchase on a federal contract can trigger consequences ranging from contract modifications to suspension. Buying from a domestic cord manufacturer reduces that risk to near zero.

Lead Times and Supply Chain Resilience

The logistics of importing electrical cords involve ocean freight, customs clearance, port congestion variability, and multi-week transit times. Under normal conditions, a standard order from an Asian cord manufacturer takes 8 to 16 weeks from purchase order to delivery. During supply chain disruptions — which have become more frequent and more severe over the past several years — those timelines can stretch to 20 weeks or longer.

A domestic cord manufacturer operates on a fundamentally different timeline. At Autac, most standard made in USA coiled cords ship same-day or within 1 to 3 business days from our Connecticut facility. We maintain inventory on over 400 standard part numbers specifically to support rapid fulfillment. Custom orders — non-standard lengths, gauges, conductor counts, jacket materials, or termination configurations — typically ship within 2 to 4 weeks, not 2 to 4 months.

This difference matters most when it matters most: when a production line is waiting on cords to complete an assembly, when a hospital needs replacement cords for patient monitoring equipment, or when a defense contractor has a delivery deadline tied to a prime contract milestone. The cost of a two-day lead time versus a twelve-week lead time is not captured in the per-unit price, but it can dwarf the unit cost difference many times over when you factor in idle labor, missed shipments, and contract penalties.

Supply chain resilience is the less visible but equally important factor. A domestic supply chain for electrical cords eliminates exposure to port strikes, container shortages, trade policy shifts, tariff changes, and international shipping disruptions. Your cord supplier's ability to deliver is not contingent on events at the Port of Long Beach or policy decisions in a foreign capital. The raw materials, the manufacturing capacity, and the shipping infrastructure are all within the continental United States.

Communication and Custom Work

Electrical cords are frequently specified as custom products. A buyer needs a specific wire gauge, conductor count, retracted length, extended length, jacket material, jacket color, plug configuration, and connector type. Many orders also involve specific UL listing requirements, custom print legends on the jacket, or non-standard packaging for integration into a larger assembly process.

Communicating these specifications accurately is critical. A misunderstood dimension, an incorrect plug type, or a wrong jacket material can result in an entire production run of unusable product. When your domestic cord manufacturer is in the same time zone, speaks the same technical language, and can review drawings and specifications in real time during business hours, the probability of specification errors drops substantially.

At Autac, our engineering and sales teams work directly with buyers to spec custom cords. There is no translation layer, no 12-hour time zone offset, and no intermediary trading company between you and the people who will manufacture your product. If a question comes up about a drawing or a material spec, it gets resolved in a phone call or an email exchange that same day. For complex custom orders, this direct communication is not a convenience — it is a safeguard against expensive errors.

The same principle applies to prototyping and iteration. When a new product design requires a custom retractile cord, the development cycle benefits enormously from a short feedback loop. We can produce prototype quantities, ship them for testing, receive feedback, and iterate on the design within weeks rather than the months required for an overseas prototype cycle. For OEM customers integrating coiled cords into their own products, this compressed development timeline directly affects time to market.

The Decline and Resurgence of U.S. Cord Manufacturing

The trend in electrical cord manufacturing over the past three decades followed the broader pattern of U.S. manufacturing: a significant shift of production to lower-cost countries, driven primarily by labor cost differentials. Many domestic cord manufacturers closed or moved production offshore during the 1990s and 2000s.

That trend has partially reversed in recent years, driven by several converging factors:

  • Rising overseas labor and shipping costs — The labor cost advantage of offshore manufacturing has narrowed as wages in major manufacturing countries have risen. Simultaneously, ocean freight costs have become more volatile and, in many periods, significantly more expensive than historical norms.
  • Supply chain disruption awareness — The disruptions of 2020–2023 fundamentally changed how procurement organizations evaluate supply chain risk. Companies that experienced months-long delays on imported components have restructured their sourcing to include or prioritize domestic suppliers.
  • Federal policy incentives — Strengthened Buy American requirements, infrastructure spending tied to domestic content provisions, and reshoring incentive programs have created direct economic advantages for domestic manufacturing.
  • Quality and liability considerations — As product liability cases involving imported electrical products have increased, buyers and their legal teams have placed greater weight on sourcing from manufacturers with clear U.S. accountability.
  • Tariff exposure — Shifting tariff policies on imported electrical goods have added cost uncertainty that makes domestic sourcing more predictable for budgeting purposes, even when the base unit cost is higher.

Through every cycle of offshoring and reshoring, we maintained our domestic production capability, our workforce, and our institutional knowledge of retractile cord engineering. That continuity is not something a company can rebuild quickly. The tooling, the process expertise, the supplier relationships, and the quality systems that produce a reliable retractile cord are built over decades, not quarters.

When you purchase American made power cords from a domestic manufacturer, the economic impact extends beyond the transaction. The wages paid to production workers, the purchases from domestic raw material suppliers, the property taxes paid to the local municipality, and the service contracts with local logistics and maintenance providers all circulate within the U.S. economy.

Autac is a small manufacturer in a small Connecticut town. Our presence supports not just our direct employees but a network of local suppliers, service providers, and community institutions. This is not unique to us — it is the reality of domestic manufacturing at every scale. The choice to buy from a U.S. manufacturer is also a choice to invest in the communities where those manufacturers operate.

For companies with corporate social responsibility commitments, ESG reporting requirements, or simply a stated preference for domestic sourcing, buying from a U.S. cord manufacturer is a straightforward way to align procurement decisions with those values. It is also increasingly a factor in B2B sales, as end customers and prime contractors ask their suppliers where their components are made.

What to Look for in a Domestic Cord Manufacturer

Not every company that sells electrical cords in the United States manufactures them here. The distinction between a domestic manufacturer and a domestic distributor of imported product is important, particularly for compliance purposes. Here is what to verify:

  • Manufacturing location — Ask where the cords are physically manufactured. A U.S. address on the invoice does not mean the product was made domestically. Ask specifically whether the winding, curing, termination, and testing happen in the United States.
  • UL listing under the manufacturer's name — A legitimate domestic manufacturer holds its own UL listing. Check the UL Product iQ database for the manufacturer's name and listing category.
  • Raw material sourcing — For BAA and Berry Amendment compliance, the origin of raw materials matters. Ask about the source of copper conductors, jacket compounds, and plug/connector components.
  • Custom capability — A company that actually manufactures can produce custom configurations. A distributor or importer typically can only offer what is in their existing catalog. The ability to modify gauge, conductor count, length, material, and termination is a strong signal of genuine manufacturing capability.
  • Direct factory communication — Can you speak directly with the engineering and production team? If your point of contact cannot answer technical manufacturing questions without consulting an overseas factory, you are likely buying imported product.
