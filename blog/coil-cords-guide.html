Coil Cords: Types, Applications, and How to Choose

Everything you need to know about coiled cords — from power and telecom to signal and control. The definitive resource from America's coil cord manufacturer.

Published May 15, 2025 12 min read

What Is a Coil Cord?

A coil cord is an electrical cable manufactured in a helical shape that stretches to a working length when pulled and retracts to a compact resting state when released. The coiled design eliminates slack, prevents tangles, and keeps cable cords organized in environments where connected devices move within a limited radius. From telephone handsets and hospital monitors to industrial pendant controllers and automotive diagnostic tools, coiled cords are one of the most versatile cable management solutions in use today.

If you have searched for coil cords, coiled cords, coiled wire, curly cords, or retractile cords, you are looking at the same fundamental product family. The terminology varies by industry and region, but the engineering is the same: a cord wire assembly with thermally set spring memory that allows it to extend and retract repeatedly over thousands of cycles. The formal industry term is retractile cord, but coil cord and coiled cord are the most widely used names in everyday specification and purchasing.

The Many Names for Coil Cords

One of the first things that confuses buyers is the sheer number of names used for this product category. A coil cord, a coiled cord, a curly cord, a retractile cord, and a coiled wire assembly are all describing the same thing — a helically wound cable that extends and retracts. Here is how the naming breaks down:

  • Coil cord / coiled cord — The most common generic terms, used across all industries. These are the terms most buyers search for when sourcing cable cords with retractile properties.
  • Retractile cord — The formal industry and UL designation. Used in engineering specifications, regulatory documents, and manufacturer catalogs. Autac's original brand name, "Re-Trak-Tul Kords," comes from this term.
  • Curly cord — Popular in telecommunications and consumer electronics. The telephone handset cord is the most recognizable curly cord, but the term applies to any coiled cable.
  • Coiled wire / coiling wires — Sometimes used when referring to the raw wire coiled into a helical form, particularly in OEM and electronics contexts where the coiled wire is integrated into a larger assembly.
  • Spiral cord — Occasionally used in European markets and some automotive applications to describe the same helical cord design.

Regardless of which name you encounter, the product is defined by its helical shape and its ability to extend under tension and retract when tension is released. When you see any of these terms, you are looking at the same core technology.

How Coil Cords Are Made

The retractile behavior of a coiled cord is not achieved by simply bending a straight cord into loops. It is engineered at the material level through a controlled thermal process that gives the jacket its permanent spring memory.

The manufacturing process begins with standard straight cord stock — insulated conductors bundled within a thermoplastic or thermoset jacket. This straight cord is wound tightly around a steel mandrel (a cylindrical form) at a precise pitch that determines the coil's diameter and spacing. The wound assembly is then placed in an oven and heat-cured at a specific temperature and duration calibrated to the jacket material. During curing, the polymer chains in the jacket material permanently rearrange into the helical configuration. Once cooled, the cord "remembers" its coiled shape at the molecular level.

The result is a wire coiled assembly that returns to its set shape after being stretched to its full working length. The straight sections at each end — called tangent leads or simply leads — are left uncoiled to provide strain relief at the plug and connector. Most coil cords have a retracted-to-extended ratio of approximately 5:1, meaning a cord with a 2-foot retracted coil length extends to roughly 10 feet of working reach.

The quality of the heat-curing process directly determines the cord's retractile performance and lifespan. Cords that are under-cured lose their spring memory prematurely. Cords that are over-cured become brittle. At Autac, we have refined our curing profiles over nearly 80 years of continuous production, and our proprietary Auta-Prene compound is specifically formulated to deliver superior retractile memory and durability in demanding environments.

Types of Coil Cords by Application

Coil cords serve a remarkably wide range of applications. While many people associate coiled cords primarily with telephone handsets or workshop extension cords, the product category is far broader. Coiled cords are manufactured for power delivery, telecommunications, data and signal transmission, equipment control, and specialized instrumentation. The table below categorizes the major coil cord types by their primary application.

Category Typical Applications Conductor Range Common Jacket Materials
Power Cords Tools, appliances, medical devices, industrial equipment 10–18 AWG, 2–3 conductors PVC (SJT), neoprene (SO, SOW), TPE
Telecom Cords Telephone handsets, headsets, intercoms, dispatch radios 22–26 AWG, 4–12 conductors Polypropylene, PVC, PUR
Data / Signal Cords Barcode scanners, POS terminals, sensors, test probes 20–26 AWG, 2–25 conductors PVC, PUR, TPE
Control Cords Pendant controllers, PLCs, CNC interfaces, hoist controls 14–22 AWG, 2–18 conductors Neoprene, PUR, TPE, Auta-Prene
Shielded Cords Medical monitors, A/V equipment, sensitive instrumentation 18–26 AWG, 2–12 conductors PVC, TPE with braided or spiral shield
Miniature Cords Handheld devices, wearable tech, compact instruments 22–28 AWG, 2–8 conductors PVC, PUR, silicone

Each of these categories has distinct electrical, mechanical, and environmental requirements. Let us look at them in more detail.

Power Coil Cords

Power coil cords are the workhorse of the product family. These are UL-listed coiled cords designed to carry AC line voltage to tools, appliances, and equipment. They use heavier gauge conductors (typically 10 to 18 AWG) and are rated for 300V or 600V depending on the cord type. SJT (Service Junior, Thermoplastic) cords handle light-to-medium duty indoor use. SO (Service, Oil-resistant) and SOW (Service, Oil-resistant, Weather-resistant) cords are built for heavy industrial, outdoor, and wet environments. A coiled power cord on a workshop bench, a hospital bed, or a commercial kitchen counter is doing the same job: delivering power while managing its own slack. For a deeper look at coiled power cords specifically, see our companion guide on coiled extension cords.

Telecommunications Coil Cords

The telephone handset cord is perhaps the most universally recognized coil cord in existence. But telecom coiled cords extend well beyond desk phones. Dispatch microphones in police and fire stations, aviation headsets, intercom stations in hospitals and schools, and two-way radio handsets all rely on coiled cords to keep communication devices connected while allowing the operator freedom of movement. These cords typically use lighter gauge coiling wires (22 to 26 AWG) with 4 to 12 conductors carrying voice, data, and control signals in a single retractile assembly.

Data and Signal Coil Cords

In retail, logistics, and manufacturing, coiled cords connect barcode scanners, POS terminals, handheld data collectors, and industrial sensors to their base stations or controllers. These cord wire assemblies carry low-voltage data signals rather than AC power, and they require clean signal transmission without interference. Conductor counts can range from 2 to 25 or more, and the coiled design keeps cables out of the way in high-traffic checkout lanes, shipping desks, and production lines.

Control Coil Cords

Pendant controllers on overhead cranes, CNC machine interfaces, and hoist control stations all use coiled cords to connect the operator's handheld control to the equipment. These cable cords must withstand constant mechanical stress — repeated extension and retraction, vibration, oil exposure, and sometimes extreme temperatures. They typically use mid-range gauge conductors (14 to 22 AWG) with multiple conductors for different control circuits, and they require heavy-duty jacket materials like neoprene or Autac's Auta-Prene compound.

Shielded Coil Cords

When electromagnetic interference (EMI) threatens signal integrity, shielded coiled cords are the solution. A braided or spiral shield layer surrounds the conductor bundle, blocking interference from nearby motors, transformers, RF sources, and other electrical noise generators. Shielded coil cords are standard in medical monitoring equipment, professional audio and video systems, laboratory instruments, and any application where data accuracy or signal quality is non-negotiable. The shielding adds some bulk and reduces flexibility slightly, resulting in a somewhat lower extension ratio compared to unshielded cords.

Miniature Coil Cords

As devices get smaller, so do the coiled cords that connect them. Miniature coil cords use fine gauge wire coiled into tight helixes with small outer diameters, making them ideal for handheld scanners, portable test equipment, wearable devices, and compact medical instruments. These cords use 22 to 28 AWG conductors and lightweight jacket materials to minimize weight and bulk while still delivering reliable retractile performance.

Key Specifications for Coil Cords

Whether you are specifying a coiled cord for a new product design or replacing an existing cord on equipment in the field, these are the parameters that matter:

  • Wire gauge (AWG) — Determines current-carrying capacity. Ranges from 10 AWG (heavy industrial) to 28 AWG (miniature signal). Heavier gauge means more amperage but a stiffer, heavier coil.
  • Conductor count — From 2 conductors (simple power or signal) to 25+ conductors (complex control and data). More conductors increase the cord's outer diameter and stiffness.
  • Voltage rating — 300V for SJT-type cords, 600V for SO/SOW-type cords. Signal and data cords may be rated at lower voltages. Always match or exceed your circuit voltage.
  • Retracted length — The compact coil length at rest, typically 1 to 10 feet. Multiply by 5 for approximate extended working length.
  • Extended length — The maximum working reach, calculated as retracted length times the extension ratio (usually 5:1) plus tangent lead lengths.
  • Tangent lead length — The straight, non-coiled section at each end, typically 6 to 12 inches. Provides strain relief and flexibility at connection points.
  • Outer diameter (OD) — The diameter of the coiled helix, which affects how the cord routes, stores, and performs in confined spaces.
  • Jacket material — PVC for indoor and light-duty use. TPE and PUR for moderate industrial. Neoprene, Auta-Prene, or SO/SOW compounds for heavy-duty, oil, chemical, and outdoor environments.
  • Temperature rating — Standard PVC operates from roughly -20°F to 150°F. Neoprene and specialty compounds extend that range significantly in both directions.
  • Shielding — Braided or spiral shield for EMI protection. Specify when the cord will be used near motors, welders, RF transmitters, or other interference sources.
  • Terminations — Plugs, connectors, stripped and tinned leads, or custom terminations. Autac can terminate coiled cords with standard NEMA plugs, modular jacks, pin connectors, or customer-specified hardware.
  • UL listing — Required for commercial power cord applications. UL categories include ZJCZ (retractile cords) and NICU (portable cord sets). Confirm your application's compliance requirements.

Industries That Rely on Coil Cords

Coiled cords are not a niche product. They serve virtually every industry where a connected device needs to move within a defined radius while maintaining a clean, organized cable connection. Here are the major sectors where coil cords are essential:

Healthcare and Medical

Patient monitors, infusion pumps, diagnostic equipment, surgical instruments, and nurse call systems all use coiled cords. In patient care environments, the self-retracting design eliminates trip hazards around beds and in hallways. Shielded coiled cords protect sensitive monitoring signals from EMI generated by nearby equipment. Medical-grade coiled cords often require biocompatibility testing and specific sterilization compatibility.

Manufacturing and Industrial

Assembly lines, CNC machining centers, overhead cranes, robotic cells, and packaging equipment all rely on coiled cords to connect handheld tools, pendant controllers, and sensors. Heavy-duty coil cords in neoprene or Auta-Prene jackets withstand oil, chemicals, abrasion, and temperature extremes common in factory environments. The retractile design keeps cord wire clear of moving machinery and conveyor paths, reducing downtime from cord damage.

Telecommunications

Beyond the ubiquitous desk phone handset cord, coiled cords serve dispatch centers, call centers, broadcasting studios, and emergency communication systems. Multi-conductor coiling wires carry voice, data, and power in a single organized assembly, simplifying station wiring.

Retail and Point-of-Sale

Barcode scanners, payment terminals, receipt printers, and customer displays connected by coiled cords stay organized at checkout counters. The cord extends when a cashier scans a large item and retracts when the scanner returns to its holder, keeping the counter clear for the next customer.

Automotive and Transportation

Diagnostic tools, trailer electrical connections, EV charging accessories, and in-vehicle communication systems use coiled cords. In automotive service bays, coiled power and data cords keep diagnostic equipment connected without draping cables across vehicle hoods and fenders. Trailer coiled cords (often called "pig tails") provide the electrical connection between the cab and trailer for lighting, braking, and ABS signals.

Food Processing and Commercial Kitchen

Countertop equipment in restaurant kitchens and food processing facilities uses coiled cords to keep cable cords off wet surfaces and out of food preparation zones. Oil-resistant and washdown-compatible jacket materials stand up to the grease, steam, and cleaning chemicals found in these environments.

Laboratory and Scientific

Test probes, analytical instruments, and laboratory equipment use coiled cords — often shielded — to maintain signal integrity in electrically noisy environments. Miniature coil cords connect handheld probes to benchtop instruments without cluttering limited bench space.

Military and Aerospace

Communication headsets, avionics connectors, and ground support equipment use mil-spec coiled cords built to withstand extreme temperature, vibration, and environmental exposure. These cords often require specialized jacket compounds, shielding configurations, and custom terminations.

How to Choose the Right Coil Cord

Selecting the right coiled cord starts with understanding your application. Work through these decision points in order:

  1. Define the electrical requirement. Is the cord carrying AC power, low-voltage DC signal, data, or a combination? Identify the voltage, amperage, and conductor count your application demands. This determines wire gauge, cord type, and UL listing requirements.
  2. Determine the working distance. Measure from the fixed connection point to the farthest position the device needs to reach. Divide by 5 to calculate the approximate retracted coil length. Add tangent lead length on each end. Build in a margin — a cord stretched to its absolute maximum extension on every cycle will fatigue faster than one working at 80% of its rated extension.
  3. Assess the environment. Indoor and climate-controlled? Standard PVC works. Outdoor, wet, oily, or chemically exposed? Specify neoprene, Auta-Prene, or SOW-rated compounds. Extreme temperatures require specialty materials with wider operating ranges. High-EMI environments need shielded wire coiled assemblies.
  4. Check compliance requirements. Commercial power applications require UL and/or cUL listed cords. Healthcare, food processing, military, and other regulated industries may have additional certification or material requirements. Confirm before specifying.
  5. Choose terminations. Standard NEMA plugs, modular connectors, stripped leads, or custom connectors? Specify the exact termination on each end. If replacing an existing cord, match the original terminations unless your application has changed.
  6. Decide: stock or custom? Autac's catalog includes over 400 standard coil cord part numbers covering the most common configurations of gauge, conductor count, length, jacket, and termination. If your application falls outside these standard options — a non-standard length, unusual conductor count, specific jacket compound, or custom termination — we manufacture custom coiled cords to your exact specifications with no minimum order quantity restrictions for most configurations.

Why Jacket Material Matters More Than You Think

The jacket is the most visible part of a coil cord, but its role goes far beyond protection. The jacket material determines the cord's retractile performance, environmental resistance, flexibility, lifespan, and compliance eligibility. Choosing the wrong jacket material is one of the most common specification errors we see.

  • PVC (polyvinyl chloride) — The most economical option. Good for indoor, climate-controlled environments with moderate mechanical stress. PVC coiled cords are the standard choice for office equipment, light-duty tools, and general-purpose applications.
  • TPE (thermoplastic elastomer) — Better flexibility and a wider temperature tolerance than PVC, with good chemical resistance. A strong middle-ground option for moderate industrial applications.
  • PUR (polyurethane) — Excellent abrasion resistance and flexibility. Common in data and signal coil cords where the cord undergoes frequent handling and bending.
  • Neoprene — The traditional heavy-duty choice. Superior oil resistance, abrasion resistance, and temperature range. Used in SO and SOW-rated coiled cords for industrial and outdoor applications.
  • Auta-Prene (Autac proprietary) — Engineered as a neoprene alternative with enhanced retractile memory and durability. Developed specifically for coil cord applications where long service life and consistent retraction performance are critical.
  • Polypropylene — Lightweight and chemical-resistant. Commonly used in telecom curly cords and low-voltage signal coiled cords.
  • Silicone — Extreme temperature range and biocompatibility. Used in medical and laboratory coiled cords where heat resistance or sterilization compatibility is required.

Stock vs. Custom Coil Cords

Many applications are well served by standard, off-the-shelf coil cord configurations. Autac's catalog of over 400 standard part numbers covers the most common combinations of wire gauge, conductor count, retracted length, jacket material, and termination type. Standard cords ship faster and cost less because they follow established manufacturing setups.

However, the versatility of coil cord manufacturing means that nearly any combination of specifications can be produced as a custom order. Common reasons for specifying a custom coiled cord include:

  • Non-standard retracted or extended lengths
  • Unusual conductor counts or gauge combinations
  • Specific jacket compounds for unique environmental conditions
  • Custom terminations or connector types
  • Special shielding configurations
  • Color-coded jackets or conductors for identification
  • Specific UL or regulatory listing requirements
