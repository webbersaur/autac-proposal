Coiled Electrical Cords and Power Cables: A Buyer's Guide

How to select the right coiled electrical cord or coiled power cable for your application — from wire gauge and cord type to jacket material and UL listing.

Published July 15, 2025 10 min read

What Is a Coiled Electrical Cord?

A coiled electrical cord is a power cable manufactured in a helical shape that stretches to reach equipment and retracts to a compact resting length when released. Unlike a straight power cord that lies in fixed loops across a floor, a coiled electric cord manages its own slack automatically. You pull it to the distance you need, and spring memory built into the jacket material brings it back when you are done.

If you have searched for a curly electrical cord, a coiled electrical wire, a curly electrical wire, or a coiled power cable, you are looking at the same category of product. The formal industry term is retractile cord, but buyers use many names — coiled electrical cable, coiled electric cord, curly power cord — and they all describe a cord with engineered spring memory that allows it to extend and retract thousands of times without permanent deformation.

This guide focuses specifically on coiled cords designed to carry AC electrical power to tools, equipment, and appliances. While coiled cords also exist for low-voltage signal, data, and communication applications, the engineering considerations for power delivery — wire gauge, amperage capacity, cord type designation, and UL safety listing — are distinct and critical to get right.

How Coiled Electrical Cords Differ from Signal and Data Cords

Not every coiled cord is built to carry power. The coiled cord on a telephone handset, for example, carries a low-voltage audio signal through fine 26 AWG conductors. A coiled USB cable transmits data. These cords are engineered for signal integrity, not current capacity.

A coiled electrical cord designed for power delivery is a fundamentally different product. The differences that matter to buyers include:

  • Heavier wire gauge — Power cords use 10 AWG to 18 AWG conductors to handle 5 to 30 amps of current. Signal cords use 22 AWG to 26 AWG conductors rated for milliamps.
  • Cord type designation — Power cords carry NEC (National Electrical Code) type designations like SJT, SJTO, SO, or SOW that specify their voltage rating, insulation class, and approved use environment. Signal cords have no such designation.
  • UL listing — Coiled power cables used in commercial and industrial settings must be UL listed to meet electrical safety standards. Signal cords typically are not UL listed for power delivery.
  • Grounding conductor — Most coiled electrical cords include a third grounding conductor (3-wire) for equipment safety. Signal cords rarely include a ground.
  • Thicker insulation and jacket — The jacket on a coiled power cable must withstand higher temperatures generated by current flow, resist abrasion in industrial environments, and meet flame-resistance standards.

When purchasing a coiled cord for a power application, confirm that the cord is specifically rated and listed for electrical power delivery — not repurposed signal or communication cord.

Understanding Cord Type Designations

Every coiled electrical cable designed for AC power carries a cord type designation defined by the National Electrical Code. These letter codes tell you exactly what a cord is rated to do and where it can be used. Here are the designations most relevant to coiled power cables:

SJT — Service Junior, Thermoplastic

The most common cord type for light-to-medium-duty indoor power applications. SJT cords are rated at 300 volts and use a thermoplastic (typically PVC) jacket. Suitable for office equipment, countertop appliances, power tools under 15 amps, and indoor workbench use. A coiled electrical cord in SJT is the standard choice for climate-controlled environments with no oil or chemical exposure.

SJTO — Service Junior, Thermoplastic, Oil-Resistant

Same voltage and construction class as SJT, with the addition of an oil-resistant outer jacket. SJTO-rated coiled electric cords are appropriate for commercial kitchens, food processing areas, and light industrial settings where the cord may contact oils, greases, or cleaning solvents.

SO — Service, Oil-Resistant

A heavy-duty cord type rated at 600 volts with a rubber or elastomer jacket that resists oil, abrasion, and moisture. SO-rated coiled power cables handle demanding industrial environments, outdoor use, and higher-amperage equipment. The step up from SJT to SO roughly doubles the voltage rating and significantly increases mechanical durability.

SOW — Service, Oil-Resistant, Weather-Resistant

The toughest standard cord type. SOW adds weather and water resistance to the SO rating, making it suitable for outdoor power delivery, wet industrial environments, and construction sites. A coiled electrical wire in SOW jacketing handles rain, UV exposure, temperature extremes, and continuous mechanical stress.

Wire Gauge and Amperage: Getting the Right Size

The wire gauge of a curly electrical cord determines how much current it can safely carry. Using an undersized gauge for your load creates heat, voltage drop, and a serious fire risk. Using an oversized gauge adds unnecessary weight and stiffness to the coil, reducing retractile performance.

The table below shows the relationship between wire gauge, maximum amperage, and typical cord type for coiled electrical cords:

Wire Gauge (AWG) Max Amperage Typical Cord Type Common Power Applications
18 AWG 7 A SJT Desk lamps, fans, monitors, chargers
16 AWG 13 A SJT, SJTO Countertop appliances, power strips, light tools
14 AWG 15 A SJT, SO Power tools, workshop equipment, medical devices
12 AWG 20 A SO, SOW Heavy power tools, motor-driven equipment, heaters
10 AWG 30 A SO, SOW Industrial machinery, welding equipment, high-draw loads

To determine the gauge you need, check the nameplate on your equipment for its amperage draw. Then select a coiled electrical cord with a gauge rated at or above that amperage. When in doubt, go one gauge heavier — the safety margin is worth the slight increase in cord stiffness.

Also consider the extended length of the coiled cord. Longer runs increase resistance and voltage drop. For coiled power cables that extend beyond 25 feet, stepping up one wire gauge from the minimum helps maintain full voltage delivery to the connected equipment.

Jacket Materials for Different Environments

The jacket is the outer layer that protects the conductors inside a coiled electrical cord and determines its retractile performance. Jacket material selection depends entirely on where the cord will be used.

PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

The standard jacket material for indoor curly electrical wire applications. PVC is economical, provides good electrical insulation, and works well in temperature-controlled environments between approximately -20°F and 150°F. It is the default choice for SJT-rated coiled cords used in offices, retail, and light commercial settings. Limitations: PVC stiffens in cold temperatures and degrades with prolonged oil or solvent exposure.

TPE (Thermoplastic Elastomer)

A step up from PVC in flexibility, temperature range, and chemical resistance. TPE-jacketed coiled electric cords maintain good retractile memory across a wider operating range and resist oils and cleaning chemicals better than PVC. Common in medical, laboratory, and light industrial power applications where the cord must endure regular cleaning or moderate chemical contact.

Neoprene and Neoprene Alternatives

The heavy-duty standard for SO and SOW-rated coiled power cables. Neoprene jackets resist oil, ozone, UV radiation, abrasion, and extreme temperatures. They maintain flexibility in cold environments and tolerate sustained heat from high-amperage current flow. Autac's proprietary Auta-Prene compound is engineered as a neoprene alternative that delivers equivalent environmental resistance with optimized retractile memory — the cord holds its coiled shape and retracts consistently over a longer service life than standard neoprene jackets.

Polyurethane

Extremely abrasion-resistant and flexible, polyurethane jackets excel in applications where the coiled electrical cable drags across surfaces, contacts sharp edges, or endures heavy mechanical wear. Polyurethane is common on coiled cords used with handheld industrial tools and pendant controllers on factory floors.

UL Listing and Safety Compliance

UL (Underwriters Laboratories) listing is not a marketing badge — it is a safety certification that confirms a coiled electrical cord has been independently tested for electrical insulation integrity, flame resistance, mechanical durability, and current-carrying capacity under the conditions specified on its label.

For any coiled power cable used in a commercial or industrial installation, UL listing is effectively mandatory. Building codes, OSHA workplace safety regulations, and insurance policies typically require UL-listed electrical components. Installing a non-listed coiled electric cord in a commercial facility can result in code violations, failed inspections, voided insurance, and direct liability exposure in the event of an electrical incident.

Key things to verify on a UL-listed coiled electrical cord:

  • UL mark on the cord jacket — The UL logo should be molded or printed directly on the cord, not only referenced in a product listing or datasheet.
  • Listing category — Retractile cords fall under UL category ZJCZ. Portable cord sets fall under NICU. Confirm the listing matches your product type.
  • Cord type designation — The SJT, SO, SOW, or other type code must appear on the jacket alongside the UL mark.
  • cUL mark — If you need Canadian Standards Association (CSA) compliance, look for the combined UL/cUL listing mark.
  • Conductor gauge and count — Listed on the jacket (for example, "3x14 AWG" for a 3-conductor, 14 gauge cord).

Common Power Applications for Coiled Electrical Cords

Workshops and Garages

A coiled electrical cord eliminates the tangle of straight cords around workbenches and power tools. Mount the fixed end to a wall, ceiling, or bench frame, and the working end reaches drill presses, grinders, sanders, and saws as needed. When the tool is set down, the cord retracts and clears the workspace. A 14 AWG, 3-conductor coiled electric cord in SO jacket handles most shop tool loads up to 15 amps.

Medical and Healthcare Facilities

Patient monitors, infusion pumps, exam lights, and diagnostic devices in hospitals and clinics operate within a limited radius but must move freely. A curly electrical cord provides clean, retractable power delivery that prevents trip hazards in patient care areas. In healthcare settings, cord cleanliness and chemical resistance matter — TPE or neoprene jackets withstand the regular disinfecting protocols required in clinical environments.

Commercial Kitchens and Foodservice

Countertop mixers, scales, slicers, and warming equipment in restaurant kitchens sit on stainless steel prep surfaces that get wiped down constantly. A coiled power cable keeps the cord off wet countertops and away from food prep zones. Oil-resistant SJTO or neoprene jackets stand up to the combination of grease, heat, steam, and cleaning chemicals found in commercial kitchens.

Industrial Manufacturing

On assembly lines and in manufacturing cells, coiled electrical cables connect handheld tools, pendant controllers, scanners, and portable equipment to fixed power sources. The retractile design keeps cords out of moving machinery, off conveyor paths, and away from forklifts and foot traffic. Heavy-duty 10 or 12 AWG coiled power cables in SOW jackets handle motor-driven tools and high-amperage equipment in these demanding environments.

Point-of-Sale and Retail

POS terminals, receipt printers, barcode scanners, and payment devices at checkout counters benefit from curly electrical wire connections that extend when needed and retract to keep counters organized. Lighter-gauge 16 or 18 AWG coiled cords in SJT jacket work well for the low-draw electronics typical in retail power applications.

Outdoor and Construction

Temporary power connections on construction sites, outdoor event setups, and landscape maintenance equipment need coiled electrical cords rated for weather exposure. SOW-jacketed coiled power cables provide the water resistance, UV tolerance, and mechanical durability required for outdoor power delivery. The retractile design keeps excess cord off wet ground and out of foot traffic.

How to Select the Right Coiled Power Cable

Choosing the right coiled electrical cord for a power application comes down to systematically matching the cord's specifications to your requirements. Work through these decision points:

  1. Identify the electrical load. Check the nameplate on your equipment for voltage, amperage, and wattage. This determines the minimum wire gauge and cord type you need. A 12-amp kitchen mixer needs at least a 16 AWG SJT cord. A 20-amp industrial grinder needs a 12 AWG SO or SOW cord.
  2. Measure the working distance. Determine the farthest point the equipment needs to reach from the power outlet. Divide that distance by 5 to calculate the approximate retracted coil length. Add 6 to 12 inches on each end for tangent leads (the straight sections at the plug and connector ends).
  3. Determine the environment. Indoor, climate-controlled settings allow SJT-rated PVC cords. Outdoor use requires SO or SOW ratings. Oil, chemical, or moisture exposure calls for SJTO, SO, or SOW with appropriate jacket materials. Extreme temperatures require neoprene or Auta-Prene compounds.
  4. Choose the cord type designation. Match the NEC cord type to your voltage, environment, and use case: SJT for indoor/300V, SJTO for indoor with oil exposure, SO for industrial/600V, SOW for outdoor and wet industrial.
  5. Select the jacket material. PVC for budget indoor use, TPE for moderate chemical and temperature demands, neoprene or Auta-Prene for heavy-duty industrial and outdoor environments, polyurethane for maximum abrasion resistance.
  6. Confirm UL listing. For any commercial or industrial installation, require UL and cUL listing. Verify the UL mark appears on the cord jacket itself.
  7. Decide between stock and custom.
